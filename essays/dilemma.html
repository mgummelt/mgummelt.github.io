The performances of sustaining changes are often intersecting S-curves.

First-mover is usually important for disruptive changes, but not sustaining.

Three strategies for handling product evolution:

  • retreat up market
  • stick with one market tier, and grow your product along their utility function
  • use marketing tactics to alter the market value curve

incumbants

Successful disruptive companies take the tech as given, and find a market. incumbants often take the market as given, and try to find the tech.

Incumbant orgs more focused on other incumbant rather than entrant competitors

"the most fomiddable barrier the established firms faces is that they did not want to do this."

"He noted that one of the greatest difficulties encountered by corporate entrepreneurs has been finding the right “beta test sites” where products could be interactively developed and refined with customers."

Company structures typically favor component innovations, not architecture

entrants

By the time entrants pose a threat to incumbants, entrants have a lead in design and cost.

Employees can be trained to work on different things. IBM employee can work at a startup.

It's important for a firm in an emerging market to be able to be profitable at small scale, since the market is small by definition. Large companies are bad at this.

processes

Entrants create processes optimized for their value chain. e.g. Seagate had 1-year instead of 2-year design cycles.

"one reason that many soaring young companies flame out after they go public based upon a hot initial product is that whereas their initial success was grounded in resources—the founding group of engineers—they fail to create processes that can create a sequence of hot products."
